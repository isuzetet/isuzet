# ISUZET — The Complete Guide to How the Platform Works

## 1. The Problem ISUZET Solves

Ethiopia is a land of vast distances and difficult roads. Stretching over 1.1 million square kilometers, the country presents freight challenges unlike anywhere else in Africa. The journey from Addis Ababa to Hawassa covers 275 kilometers but can take anywhere from six hours in good conditions to twelve hours during rainy season when mountain passes become treacherous mud tracks. The traditional freight market operates through an informal broker system known locally as "delala" — intermediaries who connect cargo owners with drivers through personal networks, phone calls, and word-of-mouth.

This informal system creates enormous inefficiencies and suffering for all parties. Small traders wanting to ship five quintals of grain to a regional market face impossible barriers: they must find a broker, negotiate unclear prices, wait days for the broker to accumulate enough cargo to fill a truck, and then hope the cargo actually arrives. Payment is entirely cash-based and happens only after physical delivery, creating massive trust gaps. Drivers often wait days at loading points without guaranteed work, burning fuel and time. When disputes arise — cargo damaged, delivery late, payment short — there is no recourse except unreliable local courts or community pressure.

The cash-only nature of Ethiopian freight creates particular pain. Drivers must carry thousands of Birr in cash to pay for fuel, checkpoint fees, food, and repairs along routes where banking infrastructure is sparse. Orderers must physically meet drivers to pay, losing days of business opportunity. Informal brokers charge commissions ranging from eight to fifteen percent, extracted opaquely with no transparency about true costs.

Road conditions compound these problems further. The rainy season from June through September transforms many corridors into difficult passages. The Addis-Hawassa route crosses the Rift Valley, where landslides are common. The route to Gambela involves border crossings requiring special permits. Many drivers lack real-time information about which roads are passable, leading to costly detours or dangerous attempts through flooded areas.

ISUZET was built specifically for these challenges. The platform puts escrow-based trust at the center of every transaction: orderers lock payment when posting cargo, drivers receive guaranteed offers through a transparent matching system, and money transfers automatically upon delivery confirmation. By replacing informal brokers with technology-mediated trust, ISUZET cuts days from delivery times, eliminates payment risk, and enables small traders to access professional logistics services previously available only to large companies.

## 2. Who the Platform Serves

### Orderer (Cargo Owner / Shipper)

Orderers represent the demand side of ISUZET. They range from large import-export companies moving containerized goods through regional distribution centers to individual farmers selling ten quintals of produce at market. Before ISUZET, an orderer posted cargo by calling multiple brokers, waiting for callbacks, negotiating unclear prices, and hoping someone would actually show up to collect the cargo. Payment required physical presence at delivery, creating cash security risks and scheduling nightmares.

With ISUZET, orderers open the mobile application or web portal, specify origin and destination zones, cargo type and weight, pickup timing, and any special requirements like refrigeration or fragile handling. The platform instantly calculates pricing, establishes escrow hold amounts, and matches the load to available drivers. Orderers track their cargo in real-time through GPS updates, receive automatic notifications at each milestone, and know with certainty that payment will release only upon verified delivery. The platform offers particular advantages for agricultural exporters who need guaranteed cold-chain delivery, FMCG distributors requiring regular route contracts, and small traders who previously could not access reliable freight services.

### Driver (Truck Operator)

Drivers are the backbone of Ethiopian freight. Most own their trucks outright, though substantial numbers operate under fleet owners or work as independent contractors. A typical driver before ISUZET spent hours at roadside stops waiting for brokers to bring offers, accepted prices without knowing true market rates, and constantly worried about payment. Cash carrying created security risks. Route uncertainty meant drivers often arrived at destinations only to discover no return cargo available, forcing expensive empty return trips.

ISUZET transforms the driver's experience entirely. The platform presents offers with clear pricing, destination details, cargo requirements, and estimated earnings. Drivers with smartphones use the application to accept loads, navigate routes, log checkpoints, and confirm deliveries. Those without smartphones — estimated at forty to sixty percent of the driver population — use basic feature phone access via USSD code *862#, receiving offers by SMS and confirming deliveries through simple text replies. The trust scoring system rewards reliable drivers with priority access to better loads and bonus opportunities. Drivers earn directly to mobile money accounts, eliminating cash carrying risks and payment delays.

### Fleet Owner (Truck Company)

Fleet owners manage multiple trucks and drivers, handling the business operations that individual drivers cannot manage alone. A typical fleet owner operates between two and fifty trucks, employing drivers on salary, commission, or rental arrangements. Before ISUZET, fleet owners lacked visibility into driver locations, struggled to optimize truck allocation, and handled payment reconciliation manually.

The platform provides fleet owners with comprehensive management capabilities. The dashboard shows real-time truck locations, driver availability status, pending and active loads, earnings summaries, and individual driver performance metrics. Fleet owners register trucks with complete documentation including insurance, inspection certificates, and capacity specifications. When drivers under their management complete loads, earnings flow to the fleet owner account for consolidated payout and accounting. The platform handles complex scenarios like co-owned trucks, multiple drivers per truck, and maintenance scheduling. Fleet owners also benefit from fleet-level trust scoring: the collective performance of all drivers under their management affects the fleet's platform standing.

### Community Agent (Delala)

Community agents serve as the critical bridge between the digital platform and Ethiopia's cash-based rural economy. These local entrepreneurs operate in agricultural zones, registering farmers and small traders as clients who lack smartphones or digital literacy. Before ISUZET, agents earned through informal brokering, which required accumulating cargo, finding trucks, and managing payment collections — all without technology support.

With ISUZET, agents become formal platform participants with defined responsibilities and earnings. They register clients using simplified interfaces, create loads on behalf of clients, collect cash payments including the platform service fee, and settle with the platform through mobile money. Agents earn commissions per quintal transported, typically between forty and eighty Ethiopian Birr per quintal depending on corridor demand. The platform caps each agent at fifty managed clients to ensure quality service. Agents also serve as micro-credit guarantors: when a small trader needs to borrow against future cargo deliveries, the agent vouches for them. If the trader defaults, the agent's commission payments are held as penalty, creating appropriate incentive alignment.

### Transport Cooperative

Transport cooperatives represent organized groups of truck owners and drivers who band together for collective advantage. These legally registered organizations typically comprise five to twenty members who elect a dispatcher to manage load allocation. Before ISUZET, cooperatives operated through informal coordination, lacking systematic load distribution or collective bargaining power.

ISUZET provides cooperatives dedicated dispatcher authority. When loads enter the platform, they can be routed directly to the cooperative dispatcher rather than through the general matching pool. The dispatcher reviews available member trucks, considers driver preferences and truck suitability, and assigns the load to the most appropriate member. This gives cooperatives "first family" priority without sacrificing responsiveness. Cooperative members pay a five percent commission to the cooperative rather than the standard platform commission. The cooperative carries shared liability: when a member driver causes penalties through damage, late delivery, or incidents, forty percent of the penalty applies to the cooperative's collective standing, protecting individual members from complete reputational collapse.

### Operations Admin

Operations administrators are platform staff responsible for system health, dispute resolution, fraud investigation, and emergency response. This role monitors corridor conditions, adjusts pricing parameters in real-time, investigates suspicious patterns, and manages escalation protocols. Operations staff handle medical emergencies, checkpoint detention reports, and deviation investigations. They review tier five trust promotions manually to prevent gaming. Operations administrators have tools to observe live platform metrics, view anonymized driver and orderer activity, and intervene when automated systems cannot resolve situations satisfactorily.

## 3. The Complete Load Journey

### Step One: Orderer Posts the Load

The journey begins when an orderer decides to ship cargo. They open the ISUZET application and begin by selecting origin and destination zones from the geographic network covering Ethiopia's major trade corridors. The orderer specifies cargo type from a comprehensive list including general goods, livestock, refrigerated items, hazardous materials, and special categories like fresh flowers or khat. Weight is entered in kilograms, automatically converting internally to quintals for pricing calculations. The system requires pickup date and time preferences, delivery deadlines, and any special instructions such as fragile handling, temperature requirements, or loading equipment needs.

Based on these inputs, the platform calculates several key figures. The freight price derives from base rates adjusted for distance, cargo class multipliers, seasonal factors, congestion adjustments, and any applicable premiums. The escrow amount represents the total money the orderer must lock to secure the load, typically one hundred percent of the freight cost plus estimated platform commission. Commission rates vary by load value: twelve percent for loads under five thousand Birr, ten percent for loads up to thirty thousand Birr, eight percent for loads up to one hundred thousand Birr, and six percent for loads above that threshold. The orderer funds the escrow hold through mobile money transfer or bank account, and the load enters the platform as "OPEN" awaiting driver matching.

### Step Two: Matching and Driver Selection

Once a load enters the open state, the Weighted Driver Matching engine evaluates all available drivers across multiple factors. Route familiarity receives the highest weight at twenty-two percent: drivers who have successfully completed trips on this corridor before receive significant advantages. Trust score contributes sixteen percent, rewarding drivers with proven reliability histories. On-time rate contributes eighteen percent, availability fifteen percent, proximity to pickup eleven percent, load preference match eight percent, and zone familiarity contributes smaller weights.

For drivers with fewer than three completed trips on the platform, special cold-start rules apply. These new drivers receive guaranteed offer matching for their first three loads at a ten percent premium above standard rates, with the platform absorbing the subsidy cost. For drivers with three to nine trips, cold-start defaults apply: an assigned starting trust score of fifty-five out of one hundred, an assumed seventy-five percent on-time rate, and a default route familiarity score of seventy percent. This ensures new drivers receive fair matching opportunities while building actual performance histories.

The top-scoring available drivers receive offers through their preferred notification channel. Smartphone-enabled drivers receive push notifications with full load details, pricing, and one-tap acceptance. Feature phone drivers receive SMS messages formatted for basic handsets, with instructions to reply with simple codes to accept or decline. The standard acceptance window lasts twenty minutes, though time-critical loads receive only five minutes to ensure rapid response.

### Step Three: Driver Acceptance and Trip Creation

When a driver accepts an offer, the platform immediately creates a trip record linking the load, driver, and assigned truck. The driver receives pickup location details, cargo specifications, estimated earnings after commission deduction, and any special requirements. The platform automatically blocks the driver's availability status to prevent double-booking.

If all offered drivers decline, the system automatically expands the matching pool in subsequent rounds. If no acceptance occurs within two hours, the load enters an escalation state, notifying the orderer to consider price adjustment or specification changes. Orderers with high trust scores and established payment histories can request direct booking, bypassing the general matching pool to offer loads directly to specific trusted drivers.

### Step Four: Pickup Confirmation

On the pickup date, the driver navigates to the specified location using platform-provided coordinates or their own local knowledge. Upon arrival, the driver initiates pickup confirmation through the application, USSD menu, or by contacting the orderer. The orderer or their representative verifies cargo quantity, condition, and documentation. The driver confirms cargo loaded and formally marks the trip as started, triggering GPS-based tracking and en-route monitoring.

### Step Five: En-Route Monitoring and Deviation Detection

Throughout the journey, the platform tracks driver location through multiple mechanisms. Smartphone drivers send continuous GPS updates. Feature phone drivers report location periodically through USSD or SMS, with offline syncing when coverage returns. The platform calculates expected arrival times based on current position, corridor average transit data, and real-time road condition reports from other drivers.

If a driver's position deviates more than three kilometers from the planned route, the platform requests explanation. Legitimate reasons like road closures, police redirections, or emergency fuel stops are logged without penalty. Unexplained deviations trigger escalation to operations staff for investigation. Significant deviations exceeding twenty kilometers raise incident flags affecting driver trust scores.

### Step Six: Delivery Confirmation

When the driver reaches the destination, four confirmation methods accommodate different technical and connectivity environments. The preferred method uses a one-time password: the platform sends a four-digit code to the orderer's registered phone, which the driver or recipient enters to confirm delivery. Alternative methods include GPS-validated photo capture where the driver photographs delivered cargo at the location, video confirmation through recorded voice notes with recipient names, or physical confirmation by a registered community agent.

For multi-stop deliveries where one truck serves multiple destinations sequentially, the platform calculates proportional escrow release. Each stop receives an assigned percentage of total escrow, releasing immediately upon that stop's confirmation regardless of subsequent stops. This ensures drivers accumulate earnings throughout the journey rather than waiting for complete route completion.

### Step Seven: Escrow Release and Payment

Upon successful delivery confirmation, the platform automatically releases escrow to the driver within minutes. The platform deducts its commission based on the tiered structure, transfers the remaining amount to the driver's registered mobile money account or bank account, and updates all trust scoring metrics based on delivery timing, condition, and any incidents. For fleet-affiliated drivers, payouts route through the fleet owner for consolidated settlement. For time-critical cargo delivered within deadline, additional bonus payments process simultaneously.

## 4. The Matching Engine — How Drivers Are Selected

The Weighted Driver Matching system represents the core algorithmic intelligence of ISUZET. Rather than simply matching the closest available driver, the system evaluates candidates across eight weighted factors that together predict successful delivery outcomes.

Route familiarity contributes twenty-two percent of the total score. This measures whether the driver has previously completed trips on this specific corridor. Prior successful trips indicate knowledge of checkpoint locations, fuel station positions, road conditions, and local navigation challenges. The factor decreases over time — a trip completed six months ago contributes less than one completed last week — but never fully expires.

Trust score contributes sixteen percent. This composite metric aggregates performance across on-time delivery, damage-free completion, cancellation history, dispute records, and incident frequency. Trust scores range from zero to one hundred, with thresholds defining six tiers that determine load access and pricing opportunities.

On-time rate contributes eighteen percent, measuring the percentage of the driver's completed trips that arrived within the promised delivery window. This metric directly predicts future performance and heavily influences matching priority.

Availability contributes fifteen percent. This binary factor indicates whether the driver is currently free and actively seeking loads, versus being already committed, on break, or offline. The system prevents over-commitment by excluding drivers with active pending trips from new offers.

Proximity to pickup contributes eleven percent. The platform calculates straight-line distance from the driver's current position to the pickup location, converting this into a score using distance tiers: under two kilometers earns maximum score, under five kilometers earns eighty percent, under ten kilometers earns sixty percent, under twenty kilometers earns forty percent, under fifty kilometers earns twenty percent, and beyond fifty kilometers earns zero.

Corridor familiarity contributes three percent, distinguishing general route knowledge from specific experience on the exact origin-destination pairing. Load preference match contributes eight percent, checking whether the driver has indicated preference for this cargo type or has matching truck specifications. Zone match contributes seven percent, rewarding drivers currently located in or adjacent to the pickup zone.

The algorithm includes sophisticated handling for edge cases. Blocked pairings — where drivers and orderers have mutually indicated bad prior experiences — are excluded entirely from matching. Preferred pairings — where both parties rated each other highly in previous encounters — receive a fifteen percent bonus to their final score. For multi-stop loads, drivers who have previously visited all the delivery zones receive an additional five percent zone bonus.

For time-critical cargo like khat, fresh fish, or cut flowers, the matching engine operates differently. Rather than calculating weighted scores, it identifies the three nearest available drivers and sends simultaneous five-minute offers. This aggressive approach prioritizes speed over optimization since these perishable cargoes degrade rapidly. If all three decline, the system escalates, extending offers to additional drivers in waves until acceptance occurs.

When drivers have zero history on the platform, special cold-start protection applies. The first three loads receive guaranteed matching at premium rates regardless of other factors. For drivers with three to nine completed trips, the system uses generous default values: seventy percent route familiarity, seventy-five percent assumed on-time rate, and an assigned trust score of fifty-five. These defaults give new drivers fair matching opportunities while their actual performance histories develop.

## 5. How Money Works

The financial architecture of ISUZET centers on escrow-mediated transactions that eliminate payment risk for both parties. When an orderer posts a load, they lock the full expected freight cost plus estimated platform commission into a virtual escrow account. This money remains held by the platform until delivery confirmation, then automatically releases to the driver minus commission deduction.

### Escrow Mechanics

Escrow serves as the trust bridge between orderers and drivers. Orderers fund escrow through mobile money, bank transfer, or — for registered community agents with appropriate permissions — cash collection. The escrow hold protects orderers: if delivery never occurs due to driver no-show or abandonment, the escrow returns to the orderer. It simultaneously protects drivers: once they confirm delivery through any of the approved methods, release is automatic and non-reversible.

For multi-stop loads, escrow splits proportionally between stops. When the first delivery confirms, that stop's assigned percentage releases immediately. The remaining escrow stays held for subsequent stops. If a recipient fails to confirm, the twenty-four-hour auto-release rule applies: after twenty-four hours from driver arrival, the stop's escrow releases automatically regardless of confirmation status. This prevents indefinite holding and allows drivers to continue routes confidently.

### Platform Commission

ISUZET earns revenue through tiered percentage commissions applied to successful deliveries. Loads valued under five thousand Ethiopian Birr incur a twelve percent commission. Loads from five thousand to thirty thousand Birr incur ten percent. Loads from thirty thousand to one hundred thousand Birr incur eight percent. Loads above one hundred thousand Birr incur six percent. A commission floor of one thousand five hundred Birr and ceiling of thirty thousand Birr ensure appropriate economics regardless of load value.

This commission structure reflects cost realities and value delivery. Small loads require similar operational effort as large loads, justifying higher percentage rates. Large loads generate adequate absolute revenue at lower percentages. The tiered system ensures ISUZET can serve the full market from individual farmers shipping single quintals to industrial distributors moving hundreds of tons.

### Agent Per-Quintal Commissions

Community agents earn through volume-based commissions rather than percentages. For cargo consolidated through their service, agents receive between forty and eighty Birr per quintal depending on corridor demand and cargo type. This creates appropriate incentives: agents serve more clients and consolidate cargo efficiently to maximize earnings. Agents keep eighty percent of commissions, with the platform retaining twenty percent as revenue share.

### Driver Earnings and Payment Timelines

Drivers receive their truck's assigned freight cost minus platform commission. Payouts process through multiple rails: Telebirr mobile money typically settles within minutes to hours, Chapa payment gateway handles bank transfers settling next business day, and traditional bank transfers settle within one to three business days depending on recipient bank. Fleet-affiliated drivers receive payouts through their fleet owner's consolidated settlement process, which may add additional hours for internal accounting.

### Micro-Credit Financing

The "Ye Gara Neger" micro-credit product enables small traders to borrow up to twenty thousand Ethiopian Birr to fund cargo shipments before customer payments arrive. Borrowers must have completed at least five successful ISUZET shipments to qualify. Loans carry seven-day repayment terms from disbursement. A registered community agent must serve as guarantor: if the borrower defaults, the agent's next three commission payments are held in escrow as penalty.

If default occurs, a three-day grace period allows repayment without additional penalty. After grace period expiration, the loan status changes to defaulted. The agent's guarantee triggers: their wallet receives a hold on future commission payments proportional to the defaulted amount. Agents maintain vouching authority only while their guaranteed loan default rate remains below twenty percent. Exceeding twenty percent triggers sixty-day suspension from credit guarantee eligibility. Exceeding forty percent triggers permanent ban from the credit program.

The platform maintains a monthly absorption cap of fifty thousand Ethiopian Birr across all defaults. If total defaults in a month exceed this cap, remaining defaults enter a queue for the following month's absorption. This protects platform solvency while maintaining program availability.

### Cash-on-Delivery and Alternative Payment Rails

For situations where escrow is inappropriate, ISUZET supports cash-on-delivery arrangements through registered agents. The agent collects payment from the recipient at delivery, settles with the platform via mobile money, and the platform releases to the driver through normal channels. This requires agent presence at delivery locations and carries cash handling risk, but serves markets where digital payment adoption remains limited.

Insurance products also flow through the platform. Shippers can purchase cargo insurance covering up to fifty thousand Ethiopian Birr in declared value at two percent premium rates. The platform receives twelve percent of insurance premiums as revenue share, with the remainder transferring to underwriting partners.

## 6. Multi-Stop Deliveries

Many freight movements in Ethiopia involve distributing cargo to multiple destinations from a single origin. An Addis Ababa distributor shipping flour to Hawassa, Konso, and Arba Minch requires one truck making sequential deliveries rather than three separate loads. ISUZET handles this through a sophisticated multi-stop system with three escrow release modes.

When creating a multi-stop load, orderers configure between two and ten delivery stops with individual cargo quantities, recipient contacts, time windows, and locations. The platform calculates total freight cost considering base rates, distance, weight, plus a multi-stop premium of six percent multiplied by the number of additional stops beyond the first.

### Escrow Release Modes

Three escrow modes accommodate different business needs. Full-on-final mode holds all escrow until the final stop completes, then releases the entire amount. This suits situations where payment timing matters more than cash flow. Proportional mode splits escrow evenly across all stops, releasing each stop's portion upon that stop's independent confirmation. This provides drivers steady earnings throughout the route. Orderer-configured mode allows custom percentage allocation per stop, enabling scenarios where certain stops warrant higher payment — perhaps due to access difficulty or priority cargo.

### Four Confirmation Methods

Each stop supports four confirmation methods enabling delivery verification across Ethiopia's diverse connectivity environments. The primary method uses one-time passwords: the platform sends a unique four-digit code to the recipient's registered phone, which the driver obtains and enters to confirm delivery. This works anywhere with basic SMS coverage.

Photo-GPS validation allows drivers to photograph delivered cargo with GPS coordinates embedded. The platform validates the photo location against the delivery location within a five-hundred-meter radius. This suits rural areas where recipients may not have phones or may be unavailable.

Agent confirmation involves designated community agents physically verifying delivery at the stop location. Agents confirm through their dedicated interface, triggering escrow release. Voice note confirmation allows drivers to record audio confirmations including recipient names and delivery details, suitable for areas with limited literacy or verification challenges.

The twenty-four-hour auto-release rule applies to all stops. If a driver marks arrival but the stop remains unconfirmed after twenty-four hours, the platform automatically releases that stop's escrow portion and marks the stop complete. This prevents indefinite holds when recipients are unavailable or uncooperative.

## 7. Consolidated Loads (Shared Trucks)

Small traders often lack cargo volume to justify dedicated truck charter. ISUZET solves this through community agent-coordinated consolidation, where multiple small shipments share one truck with costs distributed proportionally.

The process begins when a community agent identifies consolidation opportunity — multiple traders in their zone needing similar destinations within a compatible timeframe. The agent opens a consolidation load specifying origin zone, destination corridor, total truck capacity, collection deadline, and minimum fill percentage required before truck dispatch.

Registered traders under the agent create sub-loads specifying their individual cargo quantities and requirements. Each trader funds proportional escrow based on their weight share. The collection window typically remains open for six hours to allow accumulation. The agent monitors filling progress through their dashboard.

If minimum fill percentages are met before the deadline, the consolidation proceeds. A single truck collects from the agent's consolidation point, distributing to multiple destinations. Each trader's escrow releases upon their stop's confirmation. The agent earns per-quintal commission on total consolidated weight.

If minimum fill is not met before deadline, the consolidation faces several resolution options. The platform may absorb partial underfill costs, distribute remaining capacity costs proportionally among participating traders, or require the agent to guarantee the shortfall. The specific policy depends on corridor demand conditions and agent standing.

The no-show penalty protects consolidation timelines. If a trader commits cargo but fails to present it at collection time, they forfeit a ten percent no-show fee calculated on their committed load value and face a seven-day suspension from creating new loads. This creates appropriate incentive for commitment reliability.

## 8. Every Special Cargo Type

### Khat (Chat)

Fresh khat leaves represent Ethiopia's most time-sensitive cargo. Quality degrades measurably within six hours of harvest, making rapid delivery paramount. ISUZET handles khat through special workflows: automatic time-critical classification, five-minute acceptance windows sent to three nearest drivers simultaneously, mandatory delivery deadlines locked from posting, and twenty percent price premiums above standard rates reflecting urgency. Drivers accepting khat loads receive automated reminders en route and bonus payments of three thousand Birr for on-time delivery.

### Livestock

Livestock transport requires per-head rather than per-weight pricing. Orderers specify species — cattle, sheep, goats, poultry — head count, age class, and delivery requirements. Payment calculates per successfully delivered head, with deductions for mortalities during transit. Drivers must upload veterinary health certificates at pickup, and the platform blocks livestock departures between eleven in the morning and three in the afternoon during May through September heat restriction periods. Maximum transit hours apply: twelve hours for cattle, eight hours for sheep and goats, six hours for poultry.

Trucks must have certified livestock body types with appropriate ventilation, flooring, and penning. Temperature logging is required with incident flags for excursions exceeding thirty minutes outside safe ranges. Livestock shipments carry thirty-five percent cargo class premiums above base rates.

### Refrigerated Cargo (Cold Chain)

Dairy, fresh produce, frozen meat, and cut flowers require continuous temperature maintenance. Drivers must log temperature readings at two-hour intervals throughout transit. The platform validates readings against cargo-specific temperature ranges: frozen meat requires negative eighteen to negative one hundred degrees Celsius, dairy requires two to six degrees, fresh produce requires two to eight degrees, cut flowers require four to eight degrees.

If temperature excursions exceed thirty minutes outside specified ranges, the platform raises automatic incident flags and reduces payment by twenty to forty percent depending on severity. At delivery, the system generates cold chain compliance certificates proving continuous temperature maintenance — essential for buyer acceptance in formal retail and export channels.

Cold chain shipments carry cargo class premiums ranging from fifteen percent for fresh produce to fifty percent for cut flowers. Frozen meat and fresh fish carry forty percent premiums. Temperature excursion tolerance is thirty minutes before automatic penalty application.

### Bagged Grain, Cement, and Construction Materials

Standard bulk commodities use straightforward workflows. Bagged grain, cotton, sesame, and honey carry minimal premiums at base rates or fifteen percent above. Cement carries a ten percent premium due to weight tolerance requirements and dust handling protocols. Bagged cargo requires weight verification at pickup with five percent overload tolerance before warnings and penalty application.

### Hazardous Materials

Fuel, chemicals, and other hazardous cargo require certified drivers, certified trucks, and mandatory hazmat insurance. The platform verifies all three before allowing load matching. Drivers must complete specialized training modules. Emergency contact information is mandatory and displayed prominently. Hazmat shipments carry forty percent premiums. Any hazmat incident automatically suspends the driver from future hazmat eligibility pending investigation.

### Beverages

Bottled and canned beverages require careful handling to prevent breakage and leakage. The platform applies ten percent premiums and damage clauses for broken container percentages exceeding acceptable thresholds. Stacking protocols and load distribution requirements apply.

## 9. Time-Critical Cargo

Time-critical cargo represents a fundamentally different workflow from standard freight matching. When orderers mark loads as time-critical — automatically applied for khat, fresh fish, and cut flowers, or manually specified for urgent medical supplies, critical documents, or other priority items — the platform bypasses the standard weighted matching algorithm entirely.

Instead, the system identifies the three geographically nearest available drivers regardless of other scoring factors. Simultaneous offers go to all three with only a five-minute acceptance window — one quarter of the standard twenty-minute window. This aggressive approach prioritizes speed over optimization.

Pricing automatically includes twenty percent premiums above calculated base rates. Orderers cannot negotiate these premiums downward since time-critical loads require immediate acceptance and cannot risk negotiation delays.

If all three drivers decline the initial simultaneous offer, the system immediately escalates, sending offers to the next nearest drivers in expanding waves until acceptance occurs. Failure to match within thirty minutes triggers automatic notification to orderers that time-critical service may not be available for this load at this time.

Drivers accepting time-critical loads receive automated en-route reminders at regular intervals and bonus payments of three thousand Ethiopian Birr for deliveries completed within the locked deadline. Late deliveries forfeit this bonus and incur trust score penalties appropriate to the failure severity.

The delivery deadline is fixed at posting and cannot be extended. If drivers encounter unavoidable delays like road closures, they must report through incident channels immediately. Operations staff may adjust expectations for documented force majeure events, but discretional extensions are limited to preserve time-critical service integrity.

## 10. Feature Phone and USSD Access

Between forty and sixty percent of Ethiopian truck drivers operate without smartphones, using basic feature phones with voice and SMS capability only. ISUZET provides full platform access through USSD unstructured supplementary service data menus accessed by dialing *862#.

The USSD interface presents a hierarchical menu system navigable through numeric inputs. Upon dialing, drivers see the main menu offering five options: view active loads, report current location, confirm delivery, trigger emergency SOS, or report fuel station information.

Selecting "My Loads" displays up to three active or pending loads with origin, destination, weight, and offered price. Drivers select a load using its menu number, then choose to accept or decline. Accepting generates immediate confirmation SMS, while declining returns the load to matching pools without penalty.

Load offers also arrive proactively via SMS when drivers are not actively in USSD sessions. Messages follow a standard format: "ISUZET offer: Addis to Hawassa, eight quintals, four thousand five hundred Birr. Reply ONE to accept, TWO to decline. Expires at 14:30." Simple text replies handle acceptance without requiring menu navigation.

Location reporting allows drivers to update their current position by selecting zone codes from a list or entering coordinates manually. These updates sync when connectivity returns, allowing offline trip logging for rural routes with intermittent coverage.

Delivery confirmation through USSD enters OTP mode: drivers select the confirm delivery menu, the platform sends an OTP request to the orderer, and drivers enter the returned code to complete delivery. If the orderer is unavailable, photo-GPS and agent confirmation methods provide alternatives.

The SOS feature immediately triggers emergency protocols. Selecting SOS sends location, driver identification, and timestamp to operations centers, notifies emergency contacts on file, and creates an incident record. This works even in low-connectivity areas through SMS fallback.

Fuel reporting allows drivers to contribute to collective intelligence by reporting station fuel availability and prices. Verified reports — those confirmed by other drivers within three hours — earn two hundred Birr bonuses. Fuel intelligence helps all drivers plan routes and avoid stations without fuel.

## 11. The Trust and Reputation System

Trust scores form the core reputation mechanism enabling reliable matching in a market historically plagued by information asymmetry. Every driver and fleet owner maintains a trust score from zero to one hundred, continuously updated based on performance across multiple dimensions.

The scoring model weighs six factors for drivers. On-time delivery performance contributes twenty-eight percent, disputes contribute eighteen percent, route deviations contribute twenty percent, cancellations contribute fourteen percent, incidents contribute ten percent, and anomaly flags plus cash-on-delivery discrepancies contribute five percent each.

Fleet owners have slightly different weighting: on-time performance twenty-five percent, disputes twenty percent, deviations ten percent, cancellations twenty percent, payment reliability twenty percent, and incidents five percent. This reflects the different role fleet owners play in the ecosystem.

Trust scores decay over time — older incidents impact scores less than recent ones. A cancellation from six months ago matters far less than one from last week. This allows recovery and avoids permanent punishment for past mistakes.

Six trust tiers define platform privileges and access. Tier zero includes scores below forty: these users face restrictions including potential suspension from new loads. Tier one covers forty to fifty-four points: warned status with limited load access. Tier two covers fifty-five to sixty-nine: probation with restricted matching priority. Tier three covers seventy to seventy-nine: acceptable standing with standard matching. Tier four covers eighty to eighty-nine: reliable drivers with preferred matching priority. Tier five requires ninety or above: elite status with first pick for premium loads and five percent rate premiums on accepted offers.

Trust scores directly determine which loads receive offers. Higher-tier drivers see more offers earlier and get priority matching. Tier five drivers also gain eligibility for direct booking requests from trusted orderers, bypassing general matching pools entirely.

The owner-operator cross-restriction creates shared accountability between fleet owners and their drivers. When a driver under a fleet owner's management causes incidents, damages cargo, or cancels loads, the incident affects both the driver's individual trust score and the fleet owner's collective fleet score. Conversely, when drivers perform well, both benefit. This incentivizes fleet owners to invest in driver training and management.

Cold-start protection ensures new drivers can build trust scores despite lacking history. The first three loads receive guaranteed matching at premium rates. For subsequent trips up to nine total, generous default values apply: seventy-five percent assumed on-time rate, seventy percent route familiarity, and a starting trust score of fifty-five. Actual performance data gradually replaces these defaults.

## 12. Driver Earnings and Incentives

Beyond base freight payments, ISUZET provides multiple incentive streams rewarding behaviors that improve platform quality and driver welfare.

On-time delivery bonuses pay one hundred to five hundred Ethiopian Birr depending on trust tier for deliveries completed within the promised window. These bonuses automatically process with the main payout for verified on-time deliveries.

Damage-free bonuses pay fifty to three hundred Birr for deliveries without cargo damage claims. These require orderer confirmation and trigger automatically with successful deliveries.

This crowdsources real-time road intelligence benefiting the entire driver network.

## 13. Community Agent Full Workflow

A community agent's typical day begins at dawn when farmers bring produce for market shipment. The agent opens their ISUZET application and begins registering clients who lack smartphones or self-service capability.

Client registration requires only phone numbers — formal identification is optional for basic service, though traders seeking credit or large shipments eventually need verification. The agent maintains a portfolio of up to fifty registered clients, a cap ensuring they can provide adequate service quality.

When traders have cargo to ship, the agent creates loads on their behalf, entering cargo type, weight, destination, and pickup timing. The agent uses simplified interfaces optimized for quick entry. Orderers need not interact with the platform directly — the agent serves as their interface.

The agent collects cash payments from clients including the freight cost plus a two percent service fee. These collections accumulate throughout the day, with agents settling to the platform every two hours via mobile money transfer. The agent's digital wallet tracks collections, pending settlements, and commission earnings.

When matched loads proceed to delivery, the agent may serve as confirmation authority at rural stops, physically verifying cargo receipt and triggering escrow release through their interface. This provides reliable confirmation even where recipients lack phones.

Agent commissions accumulate per quintal transported, with settlement occurring on weekly cycles. High-performing agents processing substantial volumes earn significant supplementary income while serving their communities' logistics needs.

The agent's micro-credit guarantor role carries serious responsibility. When a registered trader requests credit, the agent evaluates their reliability and decides whether to vouch. If the agent vouches and the trader repays successfully, the relationship strengthens. If the trader defaults, the agent's next three commission payments are held — potentially thousands of Birr depending on volume. If the agent's overall default rate exceeds twenty percent, they lose credit vouching privileges for sixty days. Exceeding forty percent triggers permanent bans from credit programs.

Agent performance metrics including client satisfaction ratings, settlement timeliness, and default rates determine platform standing. Low-rated agents face deactivation review by operations staff.

## 14. Transport Cooperative Full Workflow

Transport cooperatives organize multiple truck owners into collective entities with shared dispatch authority and economies of scale.

Formation requires legal registration, minimum five member fleet owners, and election of a dispatcher who becomes the cooperative's authorized representative on the platform. The dispatcher receives specialized portal access showing loads prioritized for cooperative consideration.

When a load enters the system eligible for cooperative handling, notification goes to the dispatcher rather than through general matching pools. The dispatcher reviews load specifications, considers member truck availability and suitability, and assigns the load to a specific member driver.

This bypassing of weighted matching gives cooperatives family-first priority while maintaining load quality. The dispatcher has fifteen minutes to accept before the load returns to standard matching pools. This window ensures cooperatives can secure appropriate loads without blocking fast-moving opportunities indefinitely.

Member drivers receive loads at ninety-five percent of standard rates, with five percent flowing to the cooperative treasury for collective benefit — bulk fuel purchasing, shared maintenance facilities, or emergency support funds. Cooperative members thus slightly discount individual earnings for collective advantage.

Cooperative liability operates as shared protection: when a member driver causes penalties through damage, late delivery, or incidents, forty percent of the penalty applies to the cooperative's collective standing rather than the individual driver's score alone. This prevents individual incidents from devastating members while maintaining appropriate accountability.

Cooperatives can grow by adding new members through dispatcher-approved applications. Members can exit by giving notice, with their individual truck histories transitioning to independent status. The cooperative structure scales from minimum five members to practical maximums around twenty trucks before coordination complexity outweighs benefits.

## 15. Road Intelligence and Alerts

Drivers report road conditions through their application menu or USSD option five. Reports include GPS location, timestamp, severity rating, and optional description. When submitted, alerts immediately become visible to other drivers on the same corridor or adjacent routes. Active alerts display with six-hour validity windows, after which they expire unless refreshed.

Verification occurs when other drivers confirm the same conditions. When two or more drivers report the same alert type within three hours and three hundred meters, the system marks the alert as verified. Original reporters of verified alerts receive two hundred Birr road alert bonuses automatically.

The platform uses verified alerts for dynamic routing guidance. When navigation systems encounter verified road closure or severe flooding alerts, they suggest alternate routes if available. Drivers approaching active police checkpoint locations receive advance warning enabling compliance preparation.

Fuel station intelligence accumulates separately, building availability maps showing which stations currently have diesel, prices, and estimated queue lengths. Drivers report fuel availability along with prices, and verified reports earn fifty Birr bonuses. Incident response also uses road intelligence. When drivers trigger medical SOS or breakdown requests, the platform identifies the nearest recovery resources — mechanics, tow services, fuel delivery — based on driver-reported intelligence about service availability along corridors.

Road intelligence decay operates automatically. Alerts expire after six hours to prevent outdated information affecting routing decisions. Drivers can refresh active alerts by re-reporting conditions, extending validity if problems persist.

## 16. Safety and Emergency Protocols

### Medical SOS

When drivers experience medical emergencies, they trigger SOS through the application panic button or USSD menu option four. Activation immediately initiates a multi-channel response sequence.

The platform captures the driver's last known GPS location from several minutes before activation, since the emergency may occur in areas without live connectivity. Operations centers receive immediate alerts including driver identification, location coordinates, and contact information. Regional operations staff attempt voice contact with the driver to assess severity and determine appropriate response level.

Simultaneous notifications go to the driver's registered emergency contact if one is provided. Fleet owners receive alerts if the driver operates under fleet management. All notifications include location data enabling physical assistance if the driver can describe their situation.

Critical medical emergencies trigger additional protocols: identification of nearest medical facilities from the zone database, pre-alert to those facilities if the driver can specify needed care, and coordination with local emergency services where available.

Incident records create from SOS activation, linking to the driver's active trip if one exists. The platform prevents new load matching for that driver until the incident status clears, avoiding abandonment of confirmed cargo during emergency response.

### Checkpoint Detention

Police and customs checkpoints commonly detain trucks for inspection, documentation verification, or security concerns. When detention exceeds thirty minutes, drivers report through the incident system, specifying location, checkpoint type, expected release time, and officer contact if available.

Operations staff review such reports for legitimacy and mark incidents as "no-fault" when appropriate. This prevents trust score penalties for delays outside driver control and adjusts delivery expectations retroactively. Extended detentions exceeding two hours trigger escalation to senior operations and potential diplomatic or legal support.

### Hours of Service Monitoring

Driver fatigue causes accidents. The platform tracks driving hours through GPS movement patterns and manual logging. Advisory notifications trigger at eight hours of continuous driving, recommending rest breaks. Soft blocks engage at ten hours preventing new load assignment but allowing completion of current trips. Hard blocks at fourteen hours absolutely prevent platform use until a mandatory rest period completes.

Important safety protection: drivers currently on active trips never receive interruption regardless of hour counts. The system never forces drivers to abandon cargo mid-route due to hour limits, only preventing new trip starts until adequate rest occurs.

### Breakdown SOS

Mechanical failures receive separate handling from medical emergencies. Breakdown reports trigger recovery resource identification — nearby mechanics, tow services, spare parts suppliers. The platform facilitates driver communication with resources and may authorize expense advances for critical repairs enabling cargo completion.

## 18. Micro-Credit (Ye Gara Neger)

The Ye Gara Neger micro-credit program provides working capital access to small traders who otherwise could not finance cargo shipments before customer payment.

Eligible borrowers must have completed at least five successful ISUZET shipments without disputes or defaults. This history requirement ensures basic reliability before credit extension. First-time borrowers face maximum loan amounts of five thousand Ethiopian Birr, increasing gradually with repayment history to eventual maximums of twenty thousand Birr for highly reliable customers.

Loan terms span seven days from disbursement, designed to match agricultural trading cycles where crops ship to market and payment returns within a week. Borrowers receive funds directly to registered mobile money accounts and can immediately use them to fund ISUZET loads through escrow.

The agent guarantor system creates community enforcement. Every loan requires a registered community agent to serve as guarantor. If the borrower repays successfully, the agent suffers no consequence and gains reputation for good judgment. If the borrower defaults, the platform holds the agent's next three commission payments in escrow until the default amount is recovered or the monthly absorption cap covers the loss.

Agent consequences escalate with default rates. Agents maintaining default rates below twenty percent among their guaranteed loans continue normally. Agents exceeding twenty percent face sixty-day suspension from further credit guarantees. Agents exceeding forty percent face permanent bans from credit programs and potential deactivation from platform agent status entirely.

Borrower consequences for default include immediate suspension from new credit eligibility and trust score impacts. However, single defaults do not permanently bar borrowers from future credit. After demonstrating renewed reliability through subsequent completed shipments, borrowers can regain access. The system recognizes that agricultural trading carries inherent risks beyond borrower control.

Platform protection comes through the monthly absorption cap of fifty thousand Ethiopian Birr. If total defaults in a month exceed this amount, the platform queues additional defaults for the following month's absorption capacity. This prevents credit program expansion from threatening platform solvency while maintaining service availability.

## 19. Fraud Detection and Platform Integrity

ISUZET employs multiple fraud detection mechanisms protecting platform integrity while avoiding false positives that harm legitimate users.

Shadow broker detection identifies suspicious patterns suggesting drivers are diverting platform loads to off-platform completion. Indicators include accepting loads then immediately canceling, traveling toward destination anyway per GPS, and destination-side orderers not reposting loads — suggesting the delivery occurred privately. Three such incidents on the same corridor within three weeks trigger shadow broker flags. These require human operations review before any sanctions apply; the system never automatically suspends based on pattern detection alone.

Duplicate account detection prevents gaming of referral bonuses and trust manipulation. The system flags registrations from the same phone number, same device fingerprints, or same national identification documents. Detection triggers a forty-eight-hour hold for verification review rather than immediate rejection, recognizing legitimate scenarios like device sharing or data entry errors.

USSD security relies on shared secrets between platform and mobile network operators. Session validation using these secrets prevents session hijacking and replay attacks. SMS confirmation messages include authentication codes validating message origin.

Document expiry enforcement automatically suspends drivers and trucks with expired insurance, inspection certificates, or driver licenses. The system sends advance notifications thirty days before expiration, again at fourteen days, and again at one day. On expiration date, the affected driver or truck becomes ineligible for new load matching until renewal verification.

Suspicious refusal patterns identify potentially coordinated market manipulation. When multiple drivers in geographic proximity refuse loads simultaneously, the system flags for investigation potential coordinated supply withholding. Again, human review precedes any action.

Operations staff investigate all fraud flags, examining supporting evidence, contacting involved parties, and making determinations on sanctions ranging from warnings through temporary suspensions to permanent bans for serious or repeated violations.

## 23. The Route and Zone Network

ISUZET's geographic coverage encompasses Ethiopia's major trade corridors organized into thirty-six operational zones. These zones group related cities and surrounding areas into manageable units for pricing, matching, and operational management.

Major zones include Addis Ababa and surrounding central highlands, Hawassa and the southern corridor, Bahir Dar and Lake Tana access, Gondar and the northern highlands, Mekelle and the Tigray region, Dire Dawa and eastern access, Jimma and southwestern coffee regions, and Arba Minch and southern agricultural zones. Each zone maintains demand indexes tracking truck availability relative to load volume, which feed into pricing algorithms.

Corridors connect these zones in patterns reflecting actual Ethiopian trade flows. The Addis-Hawassa corridor handles flower exports, manufactured goods, and agricultural products. Addis-Bahir Dar serves northern highland agriculture and consumer goods distribution. The Hawassa-Moyale route provides critical logistics for Kenya border trade. Gambela connections support agricultural development in the western lowlands.

Corridors carry classifications determining matching and pricing treatment. Intercity corridors connect different urban zones with standard twenty-minute acceptance windows and standard pricing. Intracity corridors operate within single zones with proximity bonuses and faster turnaround expectations. Regional corridors serve remote areas with adjusted pricing reflecting lower demand density and longer transit times.

Seasonal multipliers vary by corridor. Mountainous corridors like those crossing the Rift Valley receive higher rainy season adjustments than flat highland routes. Border corridors to Kenya and South Sudan receive security premiums reflecting elevated risk profiles. Desert corridors to Djibouti receive heat-season adjustments for the extreme temperatures trucks endure on those routes.

Security zones designate corridors with elevated considerations. Moyale and the Kenya border, Gambela and the South Sudan border, and Jijiga and the Somali region all receive special handling. Operations staff monitor these zones more closely, and loads traversing them may require additional documentation or escort arrangements depending on current security assessments.

## 24. Fleet Owner Features

Fleet owners manage capabilities unavailable to individual owner-operators, enabled by their structural position controlling multiple trucks and drivers.

The truck registration process allows fleet owners to document their entire fleets simultaneously. Each truck requires specifications including make, model, year, capacity in kilograms, body type, fuel type, and required certificates — insurance, inspection, roadworthiness. Trucks enter the platform with individually tracked maintenance schedules driven by kilometers traveled and hours of operation. Maintenance reminders trigger seven days before scheduled service due dates.

Driver management capabilities let fleet owners register drivers under their authority, assign trucks to drivers, track individual driver performance metrics, and rotate assignments based on availability and load requirements. Fleet owners can designate primary and secondary drivers per truck, managing shifts and coverage.

Payout consolidation accumulates earnings from all fleet-affiliated drivers into the fleet owner's master account. Rather than individual driver disbursements, the platform makes single transfers to fleet owners who then handle internal payroll distribution. This simplifies accounting for fleet owners managing dozens of drivers.

Fleet-level trust scoring aggregates performance across all drivers under fleet management. When individual drivers perform well, the fleet score benefits. When incidents occur, both individual and fleet scores reflect the event. This creates appropriate incentives for fleet owners to invest in driver training, maintenance quality, and operational standards.

Owner-operator cross-restriction protects fleet owners from reputational collapse when individual drivers misbehave, but does not completely isolate them. Forty percent of individual driver penalties apply to fleet scores — substantial enough to incentivize oversight, but not devastating for occasional individual failures. Conversely, good fleet scores benefit individual drivers through association.

Fleet owners gain access to analytics unavailable to individual drivers: aggregate fleet utilization rates, earnings comparisons across drivers, route profitability analysis, and maintenance cost tracking. These tools inform business decisions about fleet expansion, route specialization, and driver retention.

## 25. Referral and Growth Mechanics

ISUZET's growth relies on organic network expansion through referral programs rewarding existing users for bringing new participants to the platform.

Every driver and fleet owner receives a unique eight-character alphanumeric referral code at registration. They share these codes with peers considering platform adoption. When new users enter referral codes during their own registration, the system establishes referral tracking records linking referrer and referred.

Three referral types exist with different trigger conditions and bonuses. Driver-to-driver referrals trigger when the referred driver completes three successful trips on the platform. Referrers receive five hundred Ethiopian Birr upon trigger. Fleet-to-fleet referrals trigger when the referred fleet owner registers two trucks. Referrers receive fifteen hundred Birr upon trigger. Agent-to-fleet referrals trigger on fleet owner registration completion, with bonuses structured similarly.

The cold-start premium serves dual purposes: it attracts new drivers with above-market rates for their first three trips, and it creates positive initial experiences generating word-of-mouth referrals. The ten percent premium above standard rates cost the platform approximately two thousand Birr per new driver, but generates lifetime value from drivers who remain active and refer others.

Bonus structures align incentives appropriately. Driver bonuses are modest, reflecting individual participation value. Fleet bonuses are larger, reflecting the greater platform value of fleet capacity. Agent bonuses recognize agents' critical role in rural market development.

The platform tracks referral chains and cohort performance. Referral sources with high long-term retention rates receive enhanced recognition and support. Referral sources generating early churn trigger gentle investigation — perhaps indicating misaligned expectations or recruitment of unsuitable participants.

Monthly absorption caps on referral bonuses prevent runaway costs during rapid growth phases. If referral volume exceeds budgeted capacity, the queue carries to following months rather than triggering automatic bonus reductions that would damage trust.
